Black holes are one of the most extreme and fascinating objects in the universe. In simple terms, they’re regions of space where gravity is so strong that nothing—not even light—can escape.

### How do they form?

Most black holes form when a very massive star runs out of fuel and collapses under its own gravity. This can happen after a supernova explosion. The remaining core gets compressed into an incredibly small, dense point.

### Key features

* **Event horizon**: This is the “point of no return.” Once anything crosses it, escape is impossible.
* **Singularity**: The center of a black hole, where matter is thought to be compressed to infinite density (our current physics breaks down here).
* **Accretion disk**: A swirling disk of gas and dust around the black hole that heats up and glows brightly as it spirals inward.

### Types of black holes

* **Stellar-mass black holes**: Formed from collapsing stars (a few to dozens of times the Sun’s mass).
* **Supermassive black holes**: Found at the centers of galaxies—millions to billions of times more massive than the Sun. For example, the Milky Way’s center hosts one called Sagittarius A*.
* **Intermediate black holes**: A middle category that scientists are still studying and confirming.

### Why are they important?

Black holes help scientists test extreme physics, especially ideas from General Relativity, which predicts their existence. They also play a major role in how galaxies form and evolve.

### A common misconception

Black holes don’t “suck in” everything around them like cosmic vacuum cleaners. If our Sun were replaced by a black hole of the same mass, Earth would keep orbiting normally—just without sunlight.
