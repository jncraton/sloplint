Black holes are regions of spacetime where gravity is so extreme that nothing — not even light — can escape once it crosses the boundary called the **event horizon**.

**How they form:** Most black holes form when a massive star (roughly 20+ times the sun's mass) exhausts its nuclear fuel and collapses under its own gravity. The core implodes into a singularity — a point of theoretically infinite density — while the outer layers explode as a supernova.

**Key regions** (shown above):

- **Singularity** — the central point where matter is crushed to infinite density and known physics breaks down
- **Event horizon** — the "point of no return," typically a few to tens of kilometers across for stellar-mass black holes
- **Photon sphere** — where light can orbit in circles, though unstably
- **Ergosphere** — a region outside the event horizon (for spinning black holes) where spacetime itself is dragged around
- **Accretion disk** — superheated gas and dust spiraling inward, glowing brilliantly in X-rays
- **Relativistic jets** — beams of plasma launched perpendicular to the disk at near-light speed, sometimes spanning thousands of light-years

**Types:** Stellar-mass black holes (~3–100 solar masses), intermediate (~100–100,000), and supermassive (millions to billions of solar masses) sitting at galactic centers — including *Sagittarius A\**, the ~4 million solar-mass black hole at the center of the Milky Way.

**What we've actually observed:** The Event Horizon Telescope captured the first image of a black hole's shadow in 2019 (M87*) and Sagittarius A* in 2022. Gravitational wave detectors (LIGO/Virgo) have detected dozens of black hole mergers.
